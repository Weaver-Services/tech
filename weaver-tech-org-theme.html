How We Build

Our technology philosophy, team structure, and growth model

This document describes how our technology organisation is structured, why each capability exists as a dedicated team, and how each person's contribution deepens from here. It is the consolidated version of conversations we have had over the past few months as the team came together.

We are not building a collection of disconnected applications. We are building a technology system — one that defines products, creates software, evolves services, derives decisions, orchestrates workflows, and improves itself. Every part of this system is active today, at different intensities. The balance shifts over time. The work has always been parallel.

All coding across every workstream — domain services, web, mobile, platform, agents — is 100% AI-assisted. Every engineer works with AI as a pair. The agentic software factory goes further: for some domain services, it achieves roughly 85% spec-to-running-system without manual prompting. That distinction matters. AI-assisted means engineers direct AI. The factory means specs go in, working systems come out, and engineers review, shape, and own the result.

Our technology architecture

This is the full picture. Six capabilities, each with a clear reason for being separate. Agents cut across all of them as orchestration. Product defines what to build; the factory generates it; platform runs it; domain services, decision intelligence, and experience deliver it.

Our capabilities and why each is a separate team

The separation follows one core principle: separate what changes independently, share what is common. Platform changes on infra timelines. Domain services change on business timelines. Experience changes on user-feedback timelines. Decision Intelligence changes on data and model timelines. Product changes on market and stakeholder timelines. Coupling them slows everyone down.

Product

The team that defines what we build and why. Product was the first team to adopt Claude Code and go fully AI-native — building PRDs, prototypes, user manuals, videos, pitch decks, and test cases, all AI-accelerated.

The output is not a document thrown over a wall. The PRD + xPRD is a human-and-machine spec in one pass — precise enough for the agentic software factory to consume directly. By the time a spec reaches engineering, it has already been validated against real assumptions, journeyed for edge cases, prototyped as a clickable artifact, and test cases have been derived.

Why a dedicated team: Product decides what to build and why. Engineering decides how. When product can independently validate an idea to working prototype without engineering capacity, the cost of being wrong drops dramatically — bad ideas die at prototype, not at deployment. Separating definition from execution also means the factory receives precise, machine-readable specs rather than ambiguous documents.

The traditional product cycle — discover, spec, design, prototype, hand off — takes weeks per iteration. An AI-native product team collapses this into days. Product was our first team to go fully AI-native, and it has redefined how fast an idea can go from problem statement to validated, engineering-ready specification.

App & Web Experience

Mobile apps, enterprise portal, dashboards, and workflow interfaces. The surfaces where borrowers, field agents, operations staff, and management experience the product.

Experience has its own disciplines — design systems, interaction patterns, performance, device-specific concerns — that are orthogonal to business logic. The portal does not calculate risk; it presents a risk score that came from Decision Intelligence via a domain service. Keeping experience separate prevents business logic from leaking into UI code and lets the experience team iterate on usability without waiting for backend releases.

Why a dedicated team: Experience changes on user-feedback and design cycles. Business logic changes on regulatory and product cycles. Coupling them makes both slower.

Spotify's squad model organised engineering around long-lived capabilities, not short-lived projects. Capability-aligned teams develop compound expertise that project teams never achieve.

Domain Services

Business capability services: LOS, lead management, BRE, collections, expense management, KYC, user service, IAM, customer service, user onboarding, incentive and payout, payment, workflow, external API layer, communication, and loan management.

These are not random APIs. They execute business capabilities. They provide contracts. They expose actions. They emit events. They connect what users experience with what the business knows.

Why a dedicated team: Each domain — lending origination, identity verification, payments, collections — has its own rules, regulations, lifecycle, and rate of change. A change to the KYC flow should not require coordinating with the payment team. Organising around domains keeps change local and accountability clear.

In 2002, Jeff Bezos issued what became Amazon's API mandate: every team must expose its data and functionality through service interfaces. No direct database reads, no shortcuts. That principle — every business capability is an independent service with a contract — is foundational to how we structure our domain layer.

Decision Intelligence

Features, models, rules, recommendations, risk signals, analytics outputs, MIS, dashboards, nudges, and decision services. This layer turns data into usable business understanding.

If a system only reports what happened, it is reporting. It is not decision intelligence. DI produces decisions, not dashboards. It does not merely describe the past — it tells you what to do next.

This arc is already visible in our own analytics system. A conversation with Watson begins as an exploratory, LLM-assisted query. The moment an insight is worth repeating, it freezes into a deterministic spec that runs on schedule at zero LLM cost — identical output every time, consumable by any downstream service. That progression from exploration to frozen, reusable signal is exactly what separates decision intelligence from reporting.

Why a dedicated team: Business signals need to be reusable across services. If risk scoring lived only inside LOS, collections could not consume it. If rules lived only inside BRE, the portal could not surface them. DI exists so business understanding is computed once and consumed wherever needed.

Netflix's recommendation engine, Uber's Michelangelo ML platform, and Airbnb's feature store all reflect the same architectural insight: decision intelligence as a reusable platform capability, not logic scattered across application code. Compute the signal once. Serve it to every consumer.

Platform

Infrastructure, data platform, structured and unstructured storage, document and media storage, analytics engine, security, CI/CD, observability, and agentic runtime.

The agentic runtime belongs here because agents need a reliable execution substrate: tool registries, memory patterns, orchestration primitives, guardrails, evaluations, runtime observability, permissions, cost controls, and deployment discipline.

Our platform philosophy is wide sandbox, firm walls — guardrails embedded in templates, policies, and infrastructure-as-code modules, not in approval queues. We treat infrastructure governance the way lending treats underwriting: risk-scored and routed based on blast radius, not manually reviewed by default.

Why a dedicated team: Infra, pipelines, CI/CD, observability, and runtime are shared concerns. If every domain team built its own, we would get incompatible logging, duplicated deployment tooling, and no coherent data layer. Platform exists so every other team builds on a reliable, consistent foundation.

Stripe treats its internal developer platform as a product with internal customers. The platform team's purpose is to make every other team faster by solving shared problems once and well. Gartner estimates 80% of software engineering organisations will establish dedicated platform engineering teams by 2026. We already have.

Agentic Software Factory

The factory creates and evolves the full technology stack: applications, services, infrastructure as code, data pipelines, models, agents, tests, verifiers, and documentation. It is not an agent factory. It is a software factory — tooling and automation that codifies our patterns and makes the repeatable parts of software creation fast and reliable.

Why a dedicated capability: The highest leverage is not only writing features. It is improving the system that creates, verifies, deploys, and evolves features. Every pattern the factory learns applies to the next service, the next agent, the next pipeline. It compounds.

Elon Musk has said repeatedly that the factory is the product — that designing the production system is harder and more valuable than designing any individual car. The same logic applies to software. The system that produces and evolves our technology stack is ultimately more valuable than any single service it produces.

These boundaries will evolve. In our early months, expect overlap — a domain team may need to make a DI-style decision before the DI pipeline exists for that signal, an experience engineer may need to touch a service directly. That is normal during formation. The dedicated teams tell us where expertise should accumulate over time, not where rigid walls must stand today.

Share what is common. Own what is specific. Compute intelligence once, consume it everywhere. Quality stays where the risk is understood.

Domain services execute. Agents orchestrate.

This distinction matters for how we think about the services we are building today and the agents we are building alongside them.

Domain services provide business execution. They expose APIs, contracts, domain actions, integrations, and transactional logic. They are the machinery of the business.

Agents orchestrate across that machinery. They plan, reason, call tools, consume decisions, coordinate steps, and move workflows forward. An agent does not replace a service. It weaves services together toward an outcome that no single service could achieve alone.

Agents are not a team. They are a capability. Creation lives with the factory. Runtime lives with the platform. Usage lives everywhere. No single team "owns agents" — every team can define, deploy, and consume them.

A concrete example: collections

A borrower misses an EMI payment. Here is what happens:

Decision Intelligence flags a delinquency — a risk signal based on repayment history, credit behaviour, and the model's recovery-probability prediction. A collections agent picks up the signal. It calls LMS to check the borrower's full repayment history — how many payments made, prior misses, outstanding amount. It calls the communication service to check what contact has already been attempted — calls made, messages sent, any responses. It applies BRE rules to determine the escalation tier — is this a first miss from a reliable borrower, or a repeating pattern? Based on the tier, it triggers the appropriate workflow — a reminder SMS for a first miss, a call assignment for a second, a field visit for a persistent default. It logs the action and updates the risk signal so DI can factor the outcome into future predictions.

No single service did all of this. The agent wove six services together into a coherent business action. Each service stayed independently owned. The agent orchestrated.

Quality lives where the risk is understood

We do not have a central QA department. That is deliberate.

Application testing, journey-level integration testing, data quality, model validation, agent evaluation, infra reliability checks, and factory verifiers are different disciplines. Each requires different tools, different expertise, and a different understanding of what failure looks like.

Google's SRE model demonstrated that reliability is best owned by the teams that operate the systems, not by a separate testing organisation. "You build it, you run it" — and you verify it.

Where we are today

Every layer is active. The engineering intensity varies, and that is by design.

Today, the heaviest investment is in domain services, app/web experience, and the platform that supports them. This is correct — we are building the business systems that make everything else possible. But we have not waited to start the other layers:

  • Product is fully AI-native — building specs, prototypes, manuals, videos, and test cases at a pace that was unthinkable a year ago.
  • The software factory has produced domain services at ~85% spec-to-running-system without manual prompting.
  • Decision Intelligence has built a model and is building the analytics platform.
  • We have agents in active development.
  • All coding across every workstream is 100% AI-assisted.

All capabilities run in parallel. What changes over time is where the weight is.

Nothing starts or stops. The system matures as a whole. Product stays consistently active throughout — always defining what comes next.

How your work deepens

This is not about replacing what you do today with something different. It is about your current work being the prerequisite for deeper, higher-leverage work.

Each advanced capability is a natural extension of foundational work — not a lateral move, not a separate discipline. It requires different technology alongside the understanding you have already developed. Nobody skips levels.

Domain understanding leads to Decision Intelligence. You cannot define useful business signals without understanding the domain deeply. The engineer who built the collections service knows which borrower behaviours actually predict repayment. That understanding is earned through building, not transferred from a textbook.

Product and data flow mastery leads to agent design. An agent that orchestrates a collections workflow needs to know the product end to end — which services hold which state, what decisions depend on which signals, where human approval is required. Without that map in your head, you cannot design an agent that does the right thing.

System design depth leads to factory contribution. The factory codifies patterns. To improve it, you need to recognise what is worth codifying. That requires reading broadly across the codebase, understanding trade-offs, and having opinions about what good looks like. This comes from sustained investment in how systems are designed, not just how features are shipped.

This compounding is already visible in how we design systems. Our conversational lending architecture has two flywheels — science evolves the assessment process, and science evolves the capture experience, and they feed each other. The same dynamic applies across every layer: depth in one area produces understanding that makes adjacent layers more effective. That cycle is the career progression story in miniature.

Your current work is not a stepping stone to the "real" work. It is the foundation that makes the next layer of contribution possible. Depth today is leverage tomorrow.

There are multiple high-value paths, not just one. An engineer who goes deep on domain modeling and becomes the authority on lending rules is on a high-value trajectory. A product manager who masters spec writing so precisely that the factory can consume their PRDs directly is on another. An engineer who develops system design instincts and shapes the factory is on yet another. All are needed. The common requirement: depth in what you are doing now is what opens the next door.

Trade-offs we knowingly accept

This structure involves real tensions. Naming them is more honest than pretending the design is frictionless.

Shared platform vs. team autonomy Platform provides consistency but can bottleneck teams that need something it does not yet support. We manage this by keeping platform responsive to demand, not by building abstractions before anyone needs them.
Agent flexibility vs. auditability Agents that reason are powerful but harder to test, debug, and explain. In lending, auditability is not optional. Our agents must be able to trace and justify their actions, not just produce outcomes.
Factory speed vs. understanding If the factory generates code that engineers do not understand, we have traded velocity for fragility. Engineers own the output. The factory accelerates judgment; it does not replace it.
Reusable intelligence vs. delivery speed Building a reusable DI signal takes longer than hardcoding logic into a service. The payoff arrives when the second and third consumers appear — and in our system, they always do.
AI-native speed vs. craft 100% AI-assisted coding and AI-native product work means we move fast. The risk is skipping the understanding that comes from doing things the hard way once. We manage this by requiring engineers to review and own all output — AI generates, humans shape.

This is a system we are building together, not a plan we are waiting to start. Every layer is active. Every team's work connects to the others. The structure exists so each of us can go deep in our area while trusting that the interfaces hold.

What shifts over time is the intensity. The foundation we are building in product, domain services, experience, and platform is what makes decision intelligence, agents, and the factory progressively more powerful.

Your depth today is your leverage tomorrow.
